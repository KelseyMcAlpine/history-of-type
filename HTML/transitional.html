History of  Typeface Design

❬               Transitional               ❭

(1700–1800)


The transitional period is named for its chronological position in typographic history, which falls between the Garalde and Didone categories. The transitional period was influenced by rationalist philosophy, the enlightenment, and Neoclassicism. Characteristics of transitional typefaces include vertical axis, systematic construction and high stroke contrast. The serifs of transitional typefaces are wider and bracketed serifs with flat bases. Transitional typefaces can be used in logo design to give a sense of establishment and stability to a company’s identity. These typefaces also work well as body text.

Baskerville


Baskerville is a transitional serif typeface designed in 1757 in England by John Baskerville. This typeface was John Baskerville’s attempt to improve the types of William Caslon. Baskerville increased the contrast between thick and thin strokes, made the serifs sharper and more tapered, and shifted the axis of rounded letters to be more upright. Additionally, the curved letterforms transformed to being more circular in shape, which allowed the characters to be more consistent. The result was a typeface that “reflected Baskerville’s ideals of perfection, where he chose simplicity and quiet refinement.” The typeface we re cut by Monotype in 1923.
